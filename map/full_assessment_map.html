    What is an MDT?


    Purpose:

    The core purpose of the MDT is to make a recommendation on eligibility for NHS Continuing Healthcare drawing on the multidisciplinary assessment of needs.

    Multidisciplinary Team:

    In accordance with regulations an MDT means a team consisting of at least:

    Two professionals who are from different healthcare professions, or
    One professional who is from a healthcare profession and one person who is responsible for assessing persons who may have needs for care and support under part 1 of the Care Act 2014.

    Whilst as a minimum requirement an MDT can comprise two professionals from different healthcare professions, the MDT should usually include:

    Both health and social care professionals, who are knowledgeable about the individual’s health and social care needs
    Where possible, have recently been involved in the assessment, treatment or care of the individual.

    It is important that those contributing to this process have the relevant skills and knowledge.

    It is best practice that where the individual concerned has, for example a learning disability, or a brain injury, someone with specialist knowledge of this client group is involved in the assessment process. Consideration should be given to the timeliness of the assessment, as it must be completed within 28 days.

    The MDT works together to:

    Collate and review the relevant information on the individual’s health and social care needs.
    Use this information to help define individual needs through the completion of the DST
    Collectively make a professional judgement about eligibility for NHS Continuing Healthcare using the Primary Health Needs test (PHN), which will be reflected in its recommendation.

    This process is known as a multidisciplinary assessment of eligibility for NHS Continuing Healthcare.

    CCG responsibilities:

    Standing Rules require that the CCG:

    As far as is reasonably practicable, must consult with the relevant local authority before making any decision about an individual’s eligibility for NHS Continuing Healthcare
    Cooperate with that local authority in arranging for such persons to participate in an MDT for that purpose.

    CCGs may use a number of approaches (e.g. face-to-face, video/tele conferencing etc.) to arranging MDT assessments in order to ensure active participation of all members as far as is possible.

    Local Authority responsibilities:

    Where the LA is consulted:

    There is a requirement for the LA to provide advice and assistance to the CCG, as far as is reasonably practicable.

    A local authority must, when requested to do so by a CCG:

    Co-operate with the CCG in arranging for persons to participate in an MDT, as long as there is an agreed process to give ample notice to the Local Authority and the process should also address what action needs to take place if the LA is not available. Consideration should be given to the timeliness of the assessment, as it must be completed within 28 days.

    The involvement of local authority colleagues, as well as health professionals in the assessment process, should facilitate the process of ongoing care planning and will make decision-making effective and consistent.

    As with any assessments that are carried out, organisations are expected to fair and transparent and focus on individuals’ needs and not their circumstances.

    Setting up an MDT


    It is the responsibility of the co-ordinator to ensure that all relevant people are invited to participate in the MDT, in the light of the Framework requirements. In particular, where appropriate, those with specialist knowledge regarding the individual’s needs should be included (National Framework 2018, paragraph 126).

    The membership of the MDT should comprise professionals who can accurately identify the needs of the individual and, where possible, have recently been involved in the assessment, treatment or care of the individual.

    As a minimum it is expected that the LA will be normally involved in the MDT process wherever they have a Care Act Assessment or other relevant information regarding the individual’s needs. The National Framework encourages a joint health and social care approach to CHC assessments wherever this is practicable, particularly where the local authority has made the referral for a CHC assessment.

    If the LA is not able to commit to attending all CHC MDTs, they should agree protocols with the CCG identifying the circumstances when they will not directly participate in the MDT process. These protocols should clarify how the CCG will consult with the LA over eligibility decisions in such circumstances. These protocols should also include how work will be prioritised if there are issues around workforce capacity, bearing in mind the minimum requirements for an MDT as set out in the National Framework.

    If it is not possible for the LA to arrange for a practitioner to attend the MDT meeting within a reasonable timeframe, consideration should be given to the use of technology (e.g. teleconference or video link) in order for them to be involved in the MDT process (with reference National Framework 2018, PG 26). Similar arrangements could be considered for other members of the MDT.

    The CCG should be flexible and give as much notice as possible to the LA when requesting ‘advice and assistance’, including assessment reports, or requesting participation in MDTs. It is expected that agreed timescales should form part of local protocols.

    A prompt response from the local authority is important in order to help ensure that most eligibility decisions can be made within a 28 day timescale (National Framework 2018, paragraph 156).

    The LA should establish systems to enable it to participate in MDTs in a timely manner. For example, through a pre-planned schedule of availability of staff.

    When convening an MDT consideration should be given as to whether the individual requires, or is choosing to involve, an advocate.

    Participation in an MDT


    The MDT should ensure that CHC assessment is person-centred, keeping the individual/their representative at the heart of the process throughout. It is vital that the individual’s experience of the process is as good as it can be, minimising the potential for dissatisfaction and complaint.

    The MDT process is designed to be collaborative in order to fully consider and accurately reflect the individual’s needs in the DST. Assessing eligibility for CHC is not an adversarial process and MDT members should act accordingly.

    There is no hierarchy in the MDT. All members should work collaboratively and share responsibility for the recommendation, recognising the different skills and knowledge of those involved.

    No member of the MDT is individually responsible for the recommendation of the MDT.

    The MDT recommendation must be based on a full consideration of the individual’s needs as evidenced by the written and verbal information provided, particularly from those who have direct knowledge of the individual. Sometimes it will be necessary to obtain additional evidence to ensure a full picture of the needs.

    The MDT is required to make a recommendation and must apply the Primary Health Need test as set out in the National Framework 2018 (paragraphs 54 – 66).

    Professional disagreements may well occur and can be very constructive but should be resolved in a spirit of collaboration. Where there continues to be disagreement this should be recorded in the DST.

    The individual/their representative should not be drawn into a disagreement between professionals.

    Once a recommendation has been agreed by an MDT no organisation or manager should change or dispute that recommendation afterwards unless this forms part of an agreed local dispute resolution procedure between the local authority and the CCG, or constitutes a Framework-compliant application of the National Framework requirements regarding CCG verification of eligibility recommendations.

    Characteristics of a good quality MDT Assessment


    A good quality MDT assessment is the process of gathering relevant, accurate and up to date information about an individual`s health and social care needs.

    As a minimum a good quality MDT assessment of an individual`s health and social care needs will be:

    Preceded by informed consent or appropriate best interest’s decision
    Proportionate to the situation
    Person centred
    Informed by those directly caring for the individual
    Holistic. Looking at the range of needs from different professional and individual view points
    Taking into account differing professional views and reaching a common agreement
    Considerate of the impact of the individual’s needs on others
    Focused on improved outcomes for the individual
    Evidenced based
    Clear about needs requiring support
    Clear regarding the degree and nature of any risks to the individual (or others), the individuals view on these, and how best to manage the risks

Framework compliant MDT meeting undertaken, including the individual / representative and relevant professionals
